You sold $100 yesterday. How much of it is actually yours?

Most sellers answer with their product margin. Almost nobody answers with the real number — because the real number hides across four different fees, two dashboards, and an ad bill that grows every quarter. Let's do the math together, slowly, with sources. No drama, no smoke. Just the arithmetic of your own store.

## The four hands in your pocket

Every sale on Amazon pays four tolls before it pays you:

**1. The referral fee.** For most categories — including home and kitchen — Amazon takes 15% of the sale price off the top (8% in a few categories). That's the rent for the shelf. *(Source: Amazon's published "Selling on Amazon" fee schedule.)*

**2. Fulfillment (FBA).** If Amazon stores and ships your product, the pick-pack-ship fee for a typical home-goods parcel runs roughly $5–$9 per unit, depending on size and weight. *(Source: Amazon's FBA fulfillment fee schedule.)*

**3. Advertising.** Here is the quiet one. Organic visibility on the platform has been shrinking for years, so most sellers buy placement. Industry surveys put typical ad spend between 15% and 25% of sales for sellers who advertise — and most now do. *(Source: Jungle Scout, State of the Amazon Seller report.)*

**4. The small print.** Monthly storage, returns processing, the professional account, occasional long-term storage surcharges. Call it $2–$5 per $100 of sales for a lean catalog — more if inventory sits.

## The table nobody shows you

Take a $100 handmade home-goods sale, fulfilled by Amazon, supported by ads:

| Where it goes | How much |
|---|---|
| Referral fee (15%) | −$15 |
| FBA pick, pack, ship | −$5 to −$9 |
| Ads to stay visible (15–25% of sales) | −$15 to −$25 |
| Storage, returns, extras | −$2 to −$5 |
| **What reaches you** | **$46 to $63** |

That's before you pay for materials, labor, packaging, or your own time. Between $37 and $54 of every $100 — commonly more during aggressive ad pushes — is rent. *(Sources: Amazon fee schedules; Jungle Scout seller economics data.)*

And the trend line matters as much as the snapshot: Amazon has adjusted fulfillment and storage fees upward in most years since 2020. *(Source: Amazon's annual fee-change announcements.)* The rent doesn't just exist. It creeps.

## The fee that never shows up on an invoice

There's a fifth cost, and it's the expensive one: **the customer isn't yours.**

You can't email her. You can't tell her about your new collection. You can't ask what she'd love next. She belongs to the platform — you just made the thing she bought. Which means every future sale to that same person costs you the full toll again, forever.

And it means your whole income sits on infrastructure you don't control. One policy change, one competitor's complaint, one algorithm update — and your best month becomes your worst quarter. If you've ever felt that your business is one suspension away from zero, that feeling is not anxiety. It's an accurate read of the architecture.

## What the math actually says to do

Here's where most advice goes wrong. The answer is **not** "quit Amazon." Amazon pays your bills today, and walking away from working revenue is bravado, not strategy.

The answer is in the same table, read the other way. A $100 sale on your own site, paid through a standard processor, costs about $3.20 in payment fees — you keep roughly $97 before your own marketing. *(Source: Stripe's published pricing, 2.9% + $0.30.)* More importantly: that customer is now **your customer, not the platform's.** Her email is an asset you own. Her second purchase doesn't pay the toll.

So the move is *both/and*: keep Amazon running exactly as it runs today, and build your own channel next to it — step by step, audience by audience, a few hours a week. Not to escape the rent overnight, but so that every month a little more of your revenue is the $97 kind instead of the $46 kind.

Honest part: your own channel isn't free either. You pay in marketing — the emails, the content, the audience-building that the marketplace never asked you to learn. That's a real cost, paid in hours and patience: a working own-channel typically takes 8–12 weeks of runway before the first steady sales. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. The difference is that this cost *builds equity you keep* — while the toll builds Amazon.
